3.6 — Derivatives of Composite Functions

f(x)=(3x−4)2      ̸ ⁣ ⁣ ⁣ ⁣  ⟹    f′(x)=2(3x−4)f(x) = (3x - 4)^2 \nimplies f'(x) = 2(3x - 4)f(x)=(3x−4)2⟹f′(x)=2(3x−4)

In general, if hhh is a composite function of fff and ggg:

h(x)=f(g(x)).h(x) = f(g(x)).h(x)=f(g(x)).

Then,
h′(x)≠f′(g(x)).h'(x) \neq f'(g(x)).h′(x)=f′(g(x)).

Instead, in order to find the derivatives of composite functions, we need to use the chain rule.

Chain rule

Let hhh, fff, and ggg be functions where h(x)=f(g(x))h(x) = f(g(x))h(x)=f(g(x)), such that fff and ggg are differentiable.

Then, the chain rule states: h′(x)=f′(g(x))⋅g′(x)h'(x) = f'(g(x)) \cdot g'(x)h′(x)=f′(g(x))⋅g′(x).

In short,
h(x)=f(g(x))  ⟹  h′(x)=f′(g(x))⋅g′(x)h(x) = f(g(x)) \implies h'(x) = f'(g(x)) \cdot g'(x)h(x)=f(g(x))⟹h′(x)=f′(g(x))⋅g′(x)

Let h(x)=(3x−4)2h(x) = (3x - 4)^2h(x)=(3x−4)2. Find h′(x)h'(x)h′(x).

Notice that hhh is a composite function made up of two other functions.

  • Let’s call the “outer” function fff, where f(x)=x2f(x) = x^2f(x)=x2.
  • And the inner ggg, where g(x)=3x−4g(x) = 3x - 4g(x)=3x−4.

Then, h(x)=f(g(x))=(3x−4)2h(x) = f(g(x)) = (3x - 4)^2h(x)=f(g(x))=(3x−4)2.

Using the chain rule, we have that h′(x)=f′(g(x))⋅g′(x)h'(x) = f'(g(x)) \cdot g'(x)h′(x)=f′(g(x))⋅g′(x).

h′(x)=f′(g(x))⋅g′(x)=2(3x−4)⋅3=6(3x−4)\begin{aligned} h'(x) &= f'(g(x)) \cdot g'(x) \\ &= 2(3x - 4) \cdot 3 \\ &= 6(3x - 4) \end{aligned}h′(x)​=f′(g(x))⋅g′(x)=2(3x−4)⋅3=6(3x−4)​

Let h(x)=(5x−7)10h(x) = (5x - 7)^{10}h(x)=(5x−7)10. Find h′(x)h'(x)h′(x).

Again, we can break down hhh into two different functions: fff and ggg. Where:

f(x)=x10g(x)=5x−7f(x) = x^{10} \\ g(x) = 5x - 7f(x)=x10g(x)=5x−7

Then, h(x)=f(g(x))  ⟹  h′(x)=f′(g(x))⋅g′(x)h(x) = f(g(x)) \implies h'(x) = f'(g(x)) \cdot g'(x)h(x)=f(g(x))⟹h′(x)=f′(g(x))⋅g′(x).

h′(x)=f′(g(x))⋅g′(x)=10(5x−7)9⋅5=50(5x−7)9\begin{aligned} h'(x) &= f'(g(x)) \cdot g'(x) \\ &= 10(5x - 7)^9 \cdot 5 \\ &= 50(5x - 7)^9 \end{aligned}h′(x)​=f′(g(x))⋅g′(x)=10(5x−7)9⋅5=50(5x−7)9​

The chain and power rule shortcut

Where hhh and ggg are differentiable functions, and nnn is a number:

h(x)=(g(x))n  ⟹  h′(x)=n⋅(g(x))n−1⋅g′(x).h(x) = (g(x))^n \implies h'(x) = n \cdot (g(x))^{n-1} \cdot g'(x).h(x)=(g(x))n⟹h′(x)=n⋅(g(x))n−1⋅g′(x).

In other words, differentiate it like you would with the power rule. Then, multiply it by the derivative of the “inside part.”

Let h(x)=(5x−7)10h(x) = (5x - 7)^{10}h(x)=(5x−7)10. Find h′(x)h'(x)h′(x).

Where ggg is a function such that g(x)=5x−7g(x) = 5x - 7g(x)=5x−7 and n=10n = 10n=10, we have that:

h′(x)=n(g(x))n−1⋅g′(x)=10(5x−7)10−1⋅5=50(5x−7)9.\begin{aligned} h'(x) &= n(g(x))^{n-1} \cdot g'(x) \\ &= 10(5x - 7)^{10-1} \cdot 5 \\ &= 50(5x - 7)^9. \end{aligned}h′(x)​=n(g(x))n−1⋅g′(x)=10(5x−7)10−1⋅5=50(5x−7)9.​
